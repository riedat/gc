RE: Form I-140 Petition of EB2 National Interest Waiver for Dr. Matthew Mehta

Dear Sir or Madam:

It is my great pleasure to write this letter in support of Dr. Matthew Mehta's petition for immigration to the United States. I strongly support the  EB2 National Interest Waiver application for Dr. Matthew Mehta. Dr. Mehta is an outstanding young researcher who is making significant contributions to our early lung cancer diagnosis research.

Dr. Mehta received a Ph.D. degrees from University of Chicago in Biomedical Science. He is a highly intelligent, dedicated, and extraordinarily competent young scientist. Dr. Mehta currently holds a position of Research Associate in the Radiology Imaging Science (RIS) Center of the Radiology Department of Georgetown University, and has been working under my direction as part of a research team on a major early lung cancer detection project. His work has focused on developing a clinically practical Computer Aided Cancer Diagnosis (CACD) system to help radiologists in the detection and diagnosis of lung cancer during its early and curable stage. I worked with Dr. Mehta during his pre-doctoral research, served on his thesis review committee and I am now one of his supervisors. The work he is doing is of very high quality and is important for improving the quality of health care in America.

Dr. Mehta has played a major role in the design, execution, and analysis of novel and complex studies of early lung cancer detection and has already made significant contributions to the field. Dr. Wang is the author of several full-length publications in prestigious journals, manuscripts submitted for publication, and in preparation dealing with the most exciting new development of a lung cancer detection.

Lung cancer has become the most common and fatal malignancy in the United States. It killed an estimated 197,400 Americans in 2006. During the past ten years, the total number of annual lung cancer cases has climbed steadily. The 1996 figure was 155,000. In 2006, approximately 197,400 new cases were diagnosed. It has increased more than any other major malignancy. Since 1997, more women have died of lung cancer than breast cancer, which for over 40 years was the major cause of cancer death in women. Because symptoms of lung cancer often do not appear until the disease is in advanced steps, early detection is difficult, yet very crucial to the cure of this deadly disease.

The early detection and diagnosis of lung nodules in chest radiographs are among the most challenging clinical tasks performed by radiologists. Previous studies showed that radiologists fail to diagnose small lung nodules in as many as 30% of positive cases. In a 5-year screening project at Johns Hopkins Hospital for early detection of lung nodules, it has shown that the miss rate can be decreased to less than 20% if two or more radiologists work together. Thus, the development of a computer-aided diagnosis (CACD) system is able to function as an "alerting system" that points out the potential lung cancer locations, and as a "second opinion" provider that reports the possibility of being a lung cancer for each potential cancer location. 

Utilizing both digital image processing techniques and artificial neural networks (ANN), the CACD system successively perform two functions: (1) location of suspected nodule areas, also known as a "pre-scan process", on the digitized chest radiograph, and (2) differentiation of "true" nodules from "false" nodules, i.e., lung and chest structures. Our previous studies have shown that the CACD system has achieved an accuracy that can assist radiologists in detecting early lung nodules. Dr. Mehta's work represents a significant advance on previous work in this field.

Dr. Mehta's project is a most important proof of concept of the application of neural networks to early lung cancer detection on chest radiographs. The Johns Hopkins' Early Lung Cancer Detection Project consisted of approximately 10,000 prospectively enrolled high risk patients who were to be screened for the development of lung cancer with sputum cytology and PA and lateral chest radiographs for 5 years, and were then to be followed clinically for 14 years to assess long term outcomes. Steve Sweeney, M.D. and Dominic Travis, M.D. were respectively the Radiologist and Epidemiologist who directed the program. They have both agreed to serve as consultants to Dr. Mehta's project. 

I was a participating radiologist in the Hopkins' project, and I realized that the Hopkins' collection of chest radiographs would be of great value in testing Dr. Mehta's algorithm. Each of the chest radiographs in the Hopkins' project was obtained with the best available technology with careful attention to quality. Each was interpreted prospectively by two radiologists with any disagreements settled by Dr. Sweeney. In 2005, I started to inquire about the accessibility of the chest radiographs from that project, and in 2006, I was given possession of all of the approximately 100,000 films.

In order to determine whether the films were still of adequate quality for the project, I reviewed a sample of the films. We then digitized 15 cancer cases and processed them with Dr. Mehta's algorithm. In the original Hopkins' study, one radiologist detected an average of 55% of those cancers that could be seen retrospectively. In Dr. Mehta's evaluation, his program detected 68%. The long term survival from lung cancer is related to the size of the cancer at the time of detection. Dr. Mehta's results need to be confirmed on a large number of cases, and if confirmed, should result in a significant improvement in outcome of patients with early lung cancers.

The major cause of death from cancer is due to cancer spread and growth (metastasis) in organs distant from the primary tumor. Improvements in therapy will only come from a better understanding of the mechanisms and early detection. The work in my laboratory is sponsored by an RO1 Grant from the U.S. National Cancer Institute, National Institutes of Health. Dr. Mehta's development of a new and novel methods for cancer detection holds tremendous promise of better therapy for this devastating disease.

A clinically implemented CACD system can serve effectively as a second reader of chest radiographs and assist radiologists in the detection of early lung cancer. The clinical significance of Dr. Mehta's project and work is that it will help radiologists in their case finding of small and potentially curable patients with early stage lung cancer. The importance of this research to the national interest means that it should be continued by an excellent young researcher such as Dr. Mehta. 

Dr. Mehta has shown himself to be a dedicated and effective researcher. I am convinced that it is in the best interest of the United States to grant him permanent residency, thereby assuring continuation of his important research. I therefore support his National Interest Waiver application with great enthusiasm, and it is definitely in the U.S. national interest for Dr. Mehta to remain in the U.S. and to continue his research project.

I trust that this letter will serve to strengthen Dr. Mehta's case and that his petition will be granted. If there are any questions, please feel free to contact me.

Sincerely,

 

Roger Zimmerman, Ph.D

Clinical Director
Radiology Department, Georgetown University
