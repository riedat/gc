RE: Form I-140 Petition of EB2 National Interest Waiver for Dr. Ming Lin

Dear Center Director:

This letter is written in support of Dr. Ming Lin's immigration petition of EB2 the National Interest Waiver (Form I-140). I came to know Dr. Lin and his scientific research work in my capacity as a Director of the Sensors and Electron Devices Directorate, at US Army Research Laboratory (ARL). 

Dr. Lin was a Ph.D. candidate in the Electrical Engineering Department of the Stanford University. Through a student exchange program at ARL, he has been working as a Research Associate in the Electro-Optics/Znfrared (EO/IR) Image Processing Branch of the Signal and Image Processing Division under my direction since January 2004. His research is focused on the development of image/video compression algorithms for military applications such as digitization of the battlefield.

The Army Research Laboratory's Sensors and Electron Devices Directorate (SEDD) is the principal Army organization for research and exploratory development of electron device and sensor technologies for Army battlefield applications. For the acquisition of digitized information, such as the location and identification of military targets from a battle-space through limited bandwidth channels, effective image and video compression techniques are essential. These techniques are used to compress the battlefield imagery, such as Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and Forward Looking Infrared (FLIR) imagery, that contain targets. Developing efficient image/video compression techniques is also related to the performance of the Automatic Target Recognition (ATR) technique that can actually locate and identify the targets.

A sad lesson learned by our armed forces during the Operation Desert Storm is the inability to distinguish between the friendly and hostile vehicles or equipment under poor visibility conditions. Consequently, more than half of the American casualties were inflicted by the so called "friendly fires". 

Certainly, we should not let the same tragedy to occur again, To overcome similar problems in the future, we need Automatic Target Recognition systems that are effective and efficient in distinguishing enemy and friendly targets under adverse environmental conditions. Currently, a number of ATR projects related to different military applications are funded by the U.S. Army and the other branches. In the long run, these efforts could significantly contribute to the safety of our troop and the security of our nation.

Dr. Lin has become a research associate in the Electro-Optics/Infrared (EO/IR) Image Processing Branch of Army Research Laboratory (ARL). He had been assigned to work on the Face Recognition Technology (FERET) program which is sponsored by the Department of Defense Counter-drug Technology Development Program Office, with ARL serving as the technical agent. The primary mission of the FERET program is to develop automatic face recognition capabilities that can be employed to assist security, intelligence, and law enforcement personnel in the performance of their duties. 

In 2004, the major task of the FERET program, which is a government-monitored testing and evaluation of face recognition technology using standardized database and test procedures, was successfully performed. The availability of the FERET database and evaluation methodology has made a significant difference in the progress of developing accurate face-recognition algorithms.

Dr. Lin has been working on the development of the FERET program in several areas, which include the active development of algorithms, the availability of a large database of facial images, and a method for evaluating the performance of face recognition algorithms. With his knowledge and dedication, Dr. Lin has cultivated a number of successes in the FERET program. He has designed novel, standardized evaluation algorithms and has developed several innovative face recognition classifiers by integrating techniques, such as vector quantization (VQ) and principal component analysis (PCA). His recent experiments have shown some exciting results in terms of detection and classification rates. 

In order to improve the accuracy and efficiency of face recognition classifiers, Dr. Lin designed various projection-based techniques. His face recognition classifiers have proved to work well on a large database of facial images and to achieve a stabilized classification capability.

Working on the FERET project, Dr. Lin has made two important contributions. The first contribution is a creative design of the FERET database and evaluation methodology, thereby moving face recognition technology entered into a new era. The second contribution is that the FERET test has convinced the biometric community that face recognition is a key area of scientific endeavor. In all these projects, Dr. Lin has worked actively and independently.

Dr. Lin conducts the experiments thoroughly and produces appropriate results in a timely manner. He has assisted other research efforts in the EO/IR Image Processing Branch by giving insightful suggestions and sharing reusable computer source code. His knowledge and experience in computer equipment and system administration has benefited his colleagues and facilitated research activities in this branch.

The ATR projects are of great importance to ARL and the Army due to their versatility in many military activities. An effective ATR system that converts battlefield data into information for subsequent action is a critical aid for our soldiers. The army and other services have allocated sufficient resources for the development of ATR systems to maintain the technological advantages of our military forces. He has published three journal papers in prestigious IEEE Journal of Image Technology, and also presented his research results at several national and international conferences.

With the skills and creativity that Dr. Lin has demonstrated in these projects, I have no doubts that he will continue to do very well in the ATR field and in other related research areas. He deserves to continue to work under optimal circumstances, which at present, he can only find in the United States. I also believe that Dr. Lin's work will certainly benefit U.S. scientific research and military technology, hence his research efforts will benefit the security, strength, and prosperity of our nation. 

I hope this letter will fully support his petition for Permanent Resident of United States.

Sincerely yours,

 

 

Patrick Wade

Director of the Sensors and Electron Devices Directorate
US Army Research Laboratory
