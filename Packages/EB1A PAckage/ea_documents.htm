The Supporting Documents and a Complete List of
Documents Required for EB1-Extraordinary Ability Petition

1. The Supporting Documents for EB1-Extraordinary Ability Petition

An EB1-Extraordinary Ability case can be petitioned for through the USCIS Service Center which has jurisdiction over the place of an applicant's residence, and the applicant must provide several type of documents with the petition. An applicant should not send original documents; photocopies of documents are acceptable.

The supporting documentation is to establish that an applicant qualifies as an alien of extraordinary ability, and that the alien is going to be in activities using his/her extraordinary abilities. For academics or researchers, evidence is needed in the form of letters and affidavits from prominent experts who can confirm applicant's original scientific or scholarly contributions of major significance to the field. A case is strengthened by letters from outside an alien's current community and outside the U.S. The referrers should also outline their own standing in the field.

The required evidence should also prove that an alien has received a major, internationally-recognized award, or evidence of at least three of the evidence listed in "The Most Important Aspects and Qualification Requirements of EB1-Extraordinary Ability Petition." To qualify as an individual of extraordinary ability, an applicant may include the following evidence:

a) Receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field. Provide photocopies of award certificates or copies of letters with the award notification.

b) Memberships in associations in the field which require outstanding achievements of their members, as judged by recognized experts in the field. Provide copies of appointing letters.

c) Evidence of authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in professional journals, or other major media. Provide copies of articles.

d) Published material in professional or major trade publications or major media about applicant's work. Provide copies of publications.

e) Evidence of participation on a panel, or individually, as a judge of the work of others in the field. Provide photocopies of letters of appointment to editorial boards or letters of invitation to review the work of others.

f) Evidence of employment in a critical or essential capacity for organizations and establishments that have a distinguished reputation. Provide a statement that the individual has worked for other prestigious organizations fulfills this requirement, like the NIH.

g) Provide copies of certified and translated diplomas and transcripts. Only the highest degree is necessary, unless your current work or employment is based on a previous degree. 

2. A Complete List of Documents Required for EB1-Extraordinary Ability Petition

Generally, the following documents are required for an EB1-EA petition:

  • An Form I-140 petition cover letter to USCIS;
  • Filled I-140 form;
  • Filled form ETA-750 Part B, two copies;
  • Several reference letters (or recommendation letters) from experts in your field, explaining your standing as an "alien of extraordinary ability";
  • Copy of your passport;
  • Copy of your I-94 card (front and back);
  • Copy of Notice of Approval for your current visa status (H, L, J-1, F-1, etc.);
  • Your current Curriculum Vitae (or Resume) including your employment and research history (names, addresses, and exact dates of employment.);
  • Copies of college degree certificates;
  • Copy of any authorized employment card;
  • Copies of publications, presentations, abstracts, invitations to conferences for the alien;
  • Citations statistics, and copies of major articles where your research has been cited;
  • Comments on your work by other experts in the field, and requests for reprints of your publications;
  • Copies of any academic or professional awards or honors you have received;
  • Copies of your membership in professional associations;
  • Documents of your participation, either individually or on a panel, as the judge of the work of others in the field;
  • Critical reviews, advertisements, press releases, publications contracts, or endorsements;
  • Box office receipts or record, cassette, compact disk, or video sales;
  • Your spouse or children's information and documents;
