How to Prepare Your EB1-Extraordinary 
Ability Petition and Build a Strong Case

1. The Initial Preparatory Step and the Reference Letters

If an applicant can meet its requirements, the EB1-Extraordinary Ability (EB1-EA) may be the best choice for a Green Card application, due to its employment independence and available immigration visa numbers in most situations. An applicant needs to have exceptional qualifications to meet the stringent requirements of the EB1-EA, set up by USCIS to evaluate the applicant’s achievement in a field.

The initial preparatory step takes place before filing the Form I-140 petition based on EB1-Extraordinary Ability. The length of this process varies for different person. Some people may be ready for filing Form I-140 petition in weeks, while others may take several months. In the initial preparatory step, an applicant needs to work on the following issues:

  • writing a summary of your abilities and achievement in your field;

  • drafting and collecting reference letters from experts in your field;

  • preparing supporting documents such as diplomas, awards, professional memberships;

  • collecting evidence of your achievement and published papers.

The reference letters must meet certain requirements. They are not ordinary recommendation letters for employment purpose. To successfully support an EB1-EA petition, good reference letters are necessary. Some referrers may want the applicant to provide a first draft for them to begin with. Generally, three or more reference letters should be obtained. There is no requirement that an applicant must have reference letters. But due to the importance of the reference letters, it has become a recommended practice to obtain three or more of them.

A good reference letter should not simply praise the applicant to meet the requirements of the EB1-EA petition, and such a practice will result in failing to support crucial legal arguments. Drafting and revising reference letters have particular importance, because the contents of the letters are a significant part of the foundation of an EB1-EA petition. 

2. The Evidence Collection to Show Your National or International Acclaim

A petition in EB1-Extraordinary Ability must be accompanied by evidence to show that the applicant has national or international acclaim, and that his or her achievement has been recognized in the field of expertise. It is generally a good starting point to think of it in the following ways:

1) generally, you should have a number of publications in peer-reviewed journals, and have made original contributions in your field;

2) there are several experts in your field willing to testify that they believe you belong to a small percentage of people who have risen to the top of the field of your endeavor, and give specific reasons to support such a belief.

Also, consider the following additional questions for your qualification and achievements:

  • Have you ever been invited as a reviewer, referee or judge by a peer-reviewed journal in your field to review papers to be published?

  • Are there occasions your work in your field was reported by media? Certain publications in a conference or editorial with your name identified as the primary researcher would be helpful also.

  • Have you ever received a nationally or internationally recognized prize or award for professional excellence in your field? Encouragement awards, such as those for excellent student scholarship, usually do not meet the criterion.

  • Have you acquired membership in organizations in your field which requires outstanding achievements of its members?

3. Have Your Previous and Current Employers to Work With You

For EB1-EA petition, there are two ways to file a petition:

  • You can petition for yourself without your current employer's formal sponsorship. This means that you are both the petitioner and beneficiary. In such a case, you should sign the Form I-140 and the petition cover letter (not signed by your current employer); or

  • Your current employer is the petitioner, and you are the beneficiary of the petition. Because your employer is the petitioner, you can prepare all the documents and collect all the evidence, but your employer needs to sign the Form I-140 and the petition cover letter for you.

For each way of the petition, besides asking people to write reference letters for you and collect evidence as mentioned above, you may need to have your previous and current employers to help your petition. Some employers, especially large companies and major universities, have become experienced and have established a system to process the EB1-EA petition. These employers may take care of everything except to find people to write reference letters for you. 

However, in most situations, an employer will not take the responsibility of your EB1-EA petition. In such a case, your may want to persuade the employer to help your application. This means that you should do almost everything, and your employer's involvement includes providing company documents, applicant's contributions, project and research achievement, recommendation letters, and so on.

For most people, it is an advantage to work directly on your own Green Card application case in EB1-EA category, because much of the process is under your control, not under your employer or a lawyer's control. If your Green Card application is totally left to an employer, it may take long time for an employer to file a Labor Certification application and then file a Form I-140 application (and later found that there are no immigration visa numbers available in EB3 or EB2  immigration categories), when it could be done by yourself within several months without the Labor Certification. Because employers usually do not have a motivation to speed up an employee's Green Card application process.

4. The Helpful Supporting Documents Considered by USCIS

Your application package should include:

1) A cover letter: This letter brings the evidence you are presenting together. First, a cover letter should explain your field of endeavor, and also explain what criteria should be considered to determine your excellence in a field. Second, it should explain the evidence that you have provided with the application. Third, it should explain your current job, and how you keep on  updating your professional skills, and prove that you are still applying your skill that you are claiming the extraordinary abilities to benefit the United States.

2) Reference letters: The language of the reference letters should be such that it recommends you for extraordinary abilities. The person writing the letter should give an introduction about himself/herself, and then give reference to your research and professional work, its importance, and how it sets you apart from other peers. Also, it should mention how beneficial it would be for U.S. if your immigration petition is approved.

3) The supporting documents as evidence. You need to provide detailed evidence for everything that you claim in your application. 

4) Your resume.

The followings items are considered to be helpful supporting documents by USCIS:

1) Peer-reviewed presentations at academic symposia;  

2) Peer-reviewed articles in scholarly journals;  

3) Testimony from other scholars about how the applicant has contributed to an academic field;

4) A significant number of entries in a citation index which cite the alien’s work as authoritative in the field;  

5) Any documentation of participation by the alien as a reviewer for a peer-reviewed scholarly journal; 

6) Recommendation letters or testimonials from scholars describing the alien's work as outstanding. 

7) Theses, especially PhD thesis.

The following supporting documents are considered having little or no value by USCIS:

1) A book or an article written by the alien for a "vanity" press;

2) A footnoted reference to the applicant’s work that has not been evaluated;

3) An unevaluated listing in a subject matter index;

4) A negative or neutral review of the applicant’s work.

You should provide detailed evidence for everything that you will claim in your application. This means that your application package may be big and difficult to navigate. Therefore, you need to prepare and present your case in a well-organized manner. Facts and supporting documents should be assembled coherently, and you should list all the documents that you are providing. Also, there should be a flow in the presentation of the evidence, and you should make it clear that the evidence is indeed relevant. You should also keep a copy of everything you mail to USCIS.
