1) Evidence of the alien’s original scientific, scholarly research contribution of major significance to the academic field

Continuous and reliable precipitation information is crucial for a myriad of applications ranging from energy production to food safety. However, measuring precipitation is one of the most difficult challenges of meteorology, because precipitation occurs intermittently and with pronounced geographic and temporal variability. Surface observations alone are inadequate for observing precipitation over the ocean and unpopulated areas. On the other hand, satellite-based sensors can observe the entire earth.

In conjunction with comprehensive, accurate ground validation and calibration, satellites offer the only realistic prospect for accurate and continuous measurement of precipitation. As pointed out in the previous paragraphs, several meteorological satellite missions are being scheduled for the next decade and beyond. The next-generation geostationary satellite (GOES-R) series will provide significant improvements in technology over current GOES series with improved resolution (4X), faster coverage (5X), broader bands (2X), and more coverage simultaneously.

The evidence of my original scientific and scholarly research contributions of major significance to the academic field can be summarized in the following two projects that I finished on the current position at the University of California at Irvine.

Project One: Mesoscale Convective System Climatology using Geostationary Infrared Imagery

The results of this research on “Mesoscale Convective System Climatology using Geostationary Infrared Imagery” will be incorporated in the GOES-R product suite that will be produced soon after launch scheduled for next year. The aim of this project is to use a cloud tracking methodology to track Mesoscale Convective Systems (MCS) through their entire life cycle using data from the new Advanced Baseline Imager (ABI) sensor onboard GOES-R. This information could be useful for forecasting changes in MCS intensity that can be related to quantitative precipitation estimation. The incorporation of any useful findings into the operational GOES-R nowcasting algorithm and quantitative precipitation estimation program is among the expected outcomes of this project. (Exhibits 5, 6)

The modified ForTrACC methodology, fully described in my paper published at Weather and Forecasting (Exhibits 7), is the best choice to perform this task. The cloud cluster detection techniques are based on a fully configurable cloud top threshold temperature, a tracking algorithm for overlapping areas in successive images, and a forecast module for the evolution of each particular storm system in previous steps.

The modified ForTrACC methodology has also been tested over the Pacific Ocean to study the life cycle of severe-weather related clouds and its relationship with cirrus clouds embedded on them. My paper “Deep convection over the Eastern Tropical Pacific Observed by TRMM and GOES-W”, which is part of the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission (TRMM) special issue, was published by the International Journal of Meteorology and I am the first author (Exhibit 8).

Project Two: Analysis and Improvement of Satellite Derived Global Hydrological Products – Passive Microwave Applications

After I joined ESSIC at the University of California, Irvine, I also conducted a research project in the improvement of several hydrological products using passive microwave channels onboard LEO satellites, which was sponsored by National Science Foundation and NOAA.

The hydrological products like integrated Cloud Liquid Water (CLW) and Total Perceptible Water (TPW) have been produced since 1987 for global monthly rainfall estimation, by using the measurements from the Defense Meteorological Satellite Program (DMSP) series of Special Sensor Microwave Imager (SSMI). The objectives of my research on passive microwave applications are monitoring and retrieving the atmospheric and surface parameters, such as precipitation, sea ice, ocean surface wind speed, and cloud liquid water among other products using the first Special Sensor Microwave Imager/Sounder (SSMI/S).

My research focused on the application of SSMI/S channels to improve the performance of several hydrological products by significantly modifying the existing algorithms for SSMI. An inter-calibration methodology is developed for this application which is based on direct linear mapping for Simultaneous Conical Observations (SCO) between SSMI F15 and SSMI/S F16. After applying the proposed scheme, the statistical analysis performed with this SCO dataset shows lower bias and higher correlation between SSMI and SSMI/S retrievals. The time series analysis also shows that this scheme is not time dependant. This fact is a key issue for using SSMI/S retrievals to continue the SSMI-based hydrological data set. These data sets are an outstanding source of climate data to perform many research activities. General circulation and climate models, including those to perform future scenarios of climate change, could be fed with these satellite-derived rainfall retrievals.

The study of the hydrological cycle and the variability of the climate system is one of the most challenge activities in the climate research field. Based on my original research results, my paper “Evaluation and Improvement of AMSU Precipitation Retrievals”, published in the Journal of Geophysical Research-Atmospheres which is a major journal in the field of geophysics, has been widely accepted and cited in the geophysical research community. My research technique is based on a statistical-based model to correct the systematic bias in AMSU-B rainfall retrievals using a Gaussian function with multiple parameters, such as viewing angle, latitude and surface type. This methodology has been successfully transitioned into the NOAA/NESDIS environment as part of the operational Microwave Surface and Precipitation Products System (MSPPS) which provides near real-time surface precipitation products. (Exhibits 11, 12)

As an independent expert in climate diagnostics in the Department of Soil, Water, and Climate at University of Minnesota, Prof. Paul Korzeniowski wrote in his letter of reference that “The changes in the global distribution of precipitation are a crucial element to monitor climate change, and yet we depend on indirect, remotely-sensed estimates of precipitation from satellites for over 75% of the planet. Prof. Jason Beres, the Director of the Earth System Science Interdisciplinary Center (ESSIC) at the University of California, Irvine, highlighted the results of my research work in his reference letter: “In this excellent research, Dr. Silva developed an improved scheme to identify and map out global rainfall and its intensity.
