RE: 1-140 form petition in the category of EB1-Extraordinary Ability of Dr. Ming Zhou

 

Dear Sir or Madam:

It is my great pleasure to write in support of Dr. Zhou's petition for immigration to the United States, in the category of EB1-Extraordinary Ability. Dr. Zhou currently holds a position of Research Associate in the Imaging Science and Information Systems (ISIS) Center of the Radiology Department at Michigan University, and he has been working under my direction as part of a research team on a major early lung cancer detection project. 

Dr. Zhou's research work is focused on developing a clinically practical Computer Aided Cancer Diagnosis (CACD) system to help radiologists in the detection and diagnosis of lung cancer during its early and curable stage. I worked with Dr. Zhou during his doctoral research and served on his thesis review committee. His research work is excellent and he has extraordinary ability in biomedical research, and his research is important to improve the quality of health care in the United States.

Lung cancer has become the most common and fatal malignancy in the United States. It killed an estimated 199,400 Americans in 2009. During the past ten years, the total number of annual lung cancer cases has climbed steadily. The 1999 figure was 155,000. In 2009, approximately 199,900 new cases were diagnosed, and it has increased more than any other major malignancy. Since 1997, more women have died of lung cancer than the breast cancer, which for over 40 years, was the major cause of cancer death in women. Because symptoms of lung cancer often do not appear until the disease is in its advanced stage, an early detection is difficult and very crucial to the cure of this deadly disease.

The early detection and diagnosis of lung nodules in chest radiographs are among the most challenging clinical tasks performed by radiologists. Previous studies showed that radiologists fail to diagnose small lung nodules in as many as 30% of positive cases. In a 5-year screening project at Johns Hopkins University Hospital for early detection of lung nodules, it has shown that the missing rate can be decreased to less than 20% if two or more radiologists work together. Thus, the development of a Computer Aided Cancer Diagnosis system focuses on function as an "alerting system" that points out the potential lung cancer locations, and as a "second opinion" provider that reports the possibility of being a lung cancer for each potential cancer location. 

Johns Hopkins's Early Lung Cancer Detection Project is a most important proof of concept of the application of neural networks to early lung cancer detection on chest radiographs. This project consisted of approximately 10,000 prospectively enrolled high risk patients, who were to be screened for the development of lung cancer with sputum cytology and lateral chest radiographs for 5 years, and were then to be followed clinically for 14 years to assess long term outcomes. 

Dr. Zhou's excellent research work represents a significant advance on previous work in this field. Utilizing both digital image processing techniques and artificial neural networks (ANN), the CACD system successively perform two functions: 1) location of suspected nodule areas, also known as a "pre-scan process", on the digitized chest radiograph; and 2) differentiation of "true" nodules from "false" nodules.

Dr. Zhou's successful research results have shown that the CACD system can achieve an accuracy that may assist radiologists in detecting early lung nodules. I was a participating radiologist in the Hopkins' project. While working with that project, I realized that the Hopkins' collection of chest radiographs would be of great value in testing Dr. Zhou's algorithm. Last year, Dr. Zhou started to inquire about the accessibility of the chest radiographs and the films from the Hopkins' project. In order to determine whether the films have adequate quality for our project, Dr. Zhou's reviewed a sample of the films, then he digitized 35 cancer cases and processed them with his developed algorithm. In the original Hopkins' study, one radiologist detected an average of 68% of those cancers that could be seen retrospectively. In Dr. Zhou's evaluation, this number was greatly improved.

The long term survival from lung cancer is related to the size of the cancer at the time of detection. Dr. Zhou's excellent research results demonstrate significant improvement in outcome of patients with early lung cancers. A clinically implemented CACD system can serve effectively as a second reader of chest radiographs, and assist radiologists in the detection of early lung cancer. The clinical significance of Dr. Zhou's project and excellent research work is that it will help radiologists in their case finding of small and potentially curable patients with early stage lung cancer. 

Dr. Zhou's research have been published in a number of highly respected journals, such as Journal of Cancer Research and Biophysical Journal, and he has also presented his research work at several national and international conferences. Dr. Zhou's is also an active member of American Association for Cancer Research and American Society for Biochemistry, both are prestigious professional associations in the world. 

Dr. Zhou has shown his extraordinary ability in imaging science and information application. Therefore, it is definitely necessary for Dr. Zhou to remain in the U.S. and to continue his very successful research project. I believe that this letter will serve to strengthen Dr. Zhou's case and that his petition will be granted. If there are any questions, please feel free to contact me.

Sincerely,

 

 

Prof. Kevin Brody
Department of Radiology
Michigan University
