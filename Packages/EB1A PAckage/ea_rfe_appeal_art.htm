Sample Appeal Case Studies: Detailed Analysis of 
3 Denied EB1-EA Petitions for Artists or Actors after Appeal 

For EB1-EA application in arts or entertainment, it includes any field of creative activity, such as fine arts, visual arts, culinary arts, and performing arts. Extraordinary ability in arts means distinction. To demonstrate distinction, the petitioner must demonstrate that:

a) the beneficiary has been nominated for or has received a significant national or international awards or prize such as: an Academy Award, an Emmy, a Grammy, a Director's Guild Award, or 

b) the beneficiary meets at least three of the ten criteria for EB1-Extraordinary Ability immigration category.

Decisions by the Administrative Appeals Office (AAO) of the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) indicate that the extraordinary ability requirements for EB1-EA classification in arts or entertainment are being interpreted strictly. The following summarizes three unsuccessful AAO appeals for artists. 

1. Denied EB1-EA Petition for an Actor

The EB1-EA immigrant visa petition was denied by the Director of USCIS Nebraska Service Center (NSC). The petitioner is a theater company. The beneficiary is an actor. The petitioner employed him for three years at an undetermined salary. The NSC director denied the petition, finding that the petitioner had failed to establish that the beneficiary satisfies the standard for classification as an alien with extraordinary ability in the arts.

The alien beneficiary is a 32-year old citizen of Hungary. Following his graduation from the college of Dramatic and Cinematic Art in Budapest, the beneficiary joined the Szigligeti Theater of Szolnok, Hungary. He also joined the Csokonai Theater Company of Debrecen, Hungary.

1) The extraordinary ability criterion #1: Documentation of the alien's receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor

The beneficiary has neither been nominated for, nor has he been the recipient of any significant national or international awards or prizes in his field of endeavor. Initially, the petitioner did not assert that the beneficiary is the recipient or nominee of a major internationally recognized award such an Academy Award, an Emmy or a Tony. On appeal, counsel for the petitioner asserts that the awards received by the beneficiary "also fall under the category of nomination of recipient of significant national or international awards." 

The petitioner failed to submit sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the beneficiary had received any significant national or international awards, the beneficiary does not satisfy this criterion.

2) The extraordinary ability criterion #3: Published material about the alien in professional or major trade publications or other major media, relating to the alien's work in the field for which classification is sought. Such evidence shall include the title, date, and author of the material, and any necessary translation.

The petitioner provided evidence that the beneficiary has received recognition for his performances in Hungary. The NSC director determined that while the beneficiary may be known in the theater community in Hungary, there is no evidence that he has achieved national or international recognition. The director further noted that while the petitioner provided copies of partially translated reviews, it is not known whether or not the articles and reviews appeared in major newspapers, trade journals, magazines or other publications. 

The record of proceeding contains critical reviews of the beneficiary's performances that were published in local newspapers. In review, the petitioner failed to established that these critical reviews were published in major newspapers and magazines with a national or international circulation. The beneficiary does not satisfy this criterion.

3) The extraordinary ability criterion #8: Evidence that the alien has performed in a leading or critical role for organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation.

The petitioner established that the beneficiary has performed in lead roles for organizations that have a distinguished reputation, but failed to established that the beneficiary WILL perform in a lead, starring or critical role for such organization in the future. 

The petitioner submitted several critical reviews that demonstrate that the beneficiary has performed as a lead or starring participant in several production that have a distinguished reputation. The petitioner failed to provide evidence in the form of critical reviews, advertisements, publicity release, contracts or endorsements to demonstrate that beneficiary WILL perform as a lead or starring participant in production that have a distinguished reputation.

The petitioner states that the beneficiary will perform in an upcoming production for the petitioner as a "principal player." The petitioner's assertion is insufficient evidence. Simply going on record without supporting documentary evidence is not sufficient for the purpose of meeting the burden of proof in these proceedings. The beneficiary does not satisfy this criterion.

In summary, after a careful review of the entire record, AAO concluded that the petitioner has not shown that he is a person of extraordinary ability in theater.

2. Denied EB1-EA Petition for an Artist

The EB1-EA nonimmigrant visa petition was denied by the Director of USCIS Texas Service Center (TSC). The petitioner is an artist. The TSC director denied the petition, finding that the alien petitioner had failed to establish that he satisfies the standard for classification as an alien with extraordinary ability in the arts. 

The alien petitioner is an artist, who is a 34-year old native and citizen of Korea. He earned a bachelor's degree in fine arts in sculpture at Kyung Pook National University, Taegu, Korea. He completed his master's of fine arts degree at Queens College of the Community University of New York. He participated in a number of art exhibitions in the United States.

The alien petitioner has neither been nominated for, nor has he been the recipient of any significant national or international awards or prizes in the field of endeavor. According to the evidence on the record, the alien applicant has received three awards, but he failed to establish the significance of the awards.

1) The extraordinary ability criterion #1: Documentation of the alien's receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor.

The alien petitioner indicated that he received three awards, The alien petitioner failed to establish that these awards are equivalent to significant recognition for his achievements. The alien petitioner submitted eight testimonials from the alien's former students, former teachers, former and current employers, and colleagues. Although the testimonials' authors speak highly of the alien applicant and his awards, they fail to establish that he has received significant awards for recognition nationally or internationally for his achievements.

2) The extraordinary ability criterion #3: Published material about the alien in professional or major trade publications or other major media, relating to the alien's work in the field for which classification is sought. Such evidence shall include the title, date, and author of the material, and any necessary translation.

The alien petitioner submitted numerous translated articles about the exhibition in which he participated. Several articles mention that the alien beneficiary received an award, The petitioner failed to establish that the articles were published in major newspapers or trade journals. Further, the articles do not demonstrate that he has achieved national or international recognition for his achievements.

3) The extraordinary ability criterion #8: Evidence that the alien has performed in a leading or critical role for organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation.

The translated articles published in newspapers do not demonstrate that he has and will perform in a lead, starring, or critical role for organization and establishments that have a distinguished reputation. Several articles mention that the alien beneficiary was one of ten Korean artists to participate in a group exhibition in Ellicott City, Maryland. Other articles mention other exhibitions. 

The alien petitioner failed to establish that the exhibition has a distinguished reputation. Similarly, the petitioner failed to establish that he played a lead or critical role in the group exhibitions. Also, the petitioner submitted no documentation to establish that the he has either commanded a high salary or will command a high salary or other substantial remuneration in relation to others in the field.

After a careful review of the entire record, AAO concluded that the alien petitioner has not shown he is a person of extraordinary ability in the arts.

3. Denied EB1-EA Petition for an Mime Artist

The EB1-EA immigrant visa petition was denied by the Director of NSC. The alien petitioner is a mime artist. The NSC director denied the petition, finding that the alien petitioner had failed to establish that she satisfies the standard for classification as an alien with extraordinary ability in the arts.

The alien applicant is a 31-year old native and citizen of Japan. The record reflects that she trained in jazz dance at the Nakagawa Yukiko Jazz Dance Studio in Tokyo. She was a member of the Himawari Theater company in Tokyo. Three years ago, she began studying mime and physical comedy at the petitioner's mime company. She performed in the petitioner's "Reel to Real" performance at the Lincoln Center twice. She continue to perform with the petitioner's studio to perform with the petitioner's Ensemble.

1) The extraordinary ability criterion #3: Published material about the alien in professional or major trade publications or other major media, relating to the alien's work in the field for which classification is sought. Such evidence shall include the title, date, and author of the material, and any necessary translation.

The alien petitioner asserts that she has achieved national or international recognition for achievements evidenced by published materials about her in OCS News. The OCS News is a bi-weekly Japanese newspapers published in the United States with an approximate circulation of 20,000. The record contains one review and several announcements of her performances published in OCS News plus several other announcements in The New York Times, The Village Voice, and Time Out New York. The record contains a copy of a review published on the Wolf Entertainment Guide website, and a translation of a review published on the ASAHI website. 

The alien petitioner failed to establish that these items are evidence that she has achieved national or international recognition for her achievement.

2) The extraordinary ability criterion #8: Evidence that the alien has performed in a leading or critical role for organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation.

The alien petitioner asserts that she satisfies the criterion by virtue of having performed twice at the Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts, as well a other venues including the Puffin Room in Soho, New York City. The NSC director determined that the alien beneficiary was not a lead or starring participant in the Lincoln Center productions.

The Lincoln Center has a distinguish reputation, but she did not play a lead or starring role at the Lincoln Center. Rather, she was one of five performed in one segment of a full-length performance. Further, the petitioner has failed to establish that she will perform as a lead or starring participant in production or events that have a distinguished reputation.

The alien petitioner established that she has contracts to perform at a mitzvah in Queens, New York and at an elementary school in Chiba, Japan. She has a contract to perform at the opening of the Time Warner Building in New York City, and at the Chofushi Green Hall in Tokyo, Japan. The alien petitioner failed to establish that these future venues have a distinguished reputation, hence, failed to established that she satisfies this criterion.

3) The extraordinary ability criterion #10: Evidence of commercial successes in the performing arts, as shown by box office receipts or record, cassette, compact disk, or video sales.

The alien petitioner asserts that she has received commercial or critically acclaimed success. The alien petitioner argues that the performance in which she participated at the Lincoln Center was so commercially successful that it is now being promoted both nationally and internationally. The petitioner states that her performance at the Puffin Room received substantial critical acclaim in OCS News. The assertions of petitioner do not constitute evidence. 

Further, the critical review published in OSC News is abbreviated. The record contains insufficient evidence to establish that she has a record of major commercial or critically acclaimed success. The alien petitioner submitted a number of testimonials that speak highly of her talent and technique. While the alien applicant has received some recognition for her work in the field. The petitioner failed to establish that she has achieved distinction in her field of endeavor.

The alien petitioner submitted a favorable consultation from the American Guild of Musical Artists. Such consultations, however, are advisory in nature and are not binding on USCIS. After a careful review of the entire record, AAO concluded that the alien has not shown she is a person of extraordinary ability in the arts.
