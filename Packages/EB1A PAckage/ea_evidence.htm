How to Meet the Qualification Criteria and
Present Powerful Evidence for Your EB1-Extraordinary Ability Petition

1. The Evidence of a One-time Achievement 

The EB1-Extraordinary Ability is defined as "a level of expertise indicating that the individual is one of a small percentage who have risen to the very top of the field of endeavor." The law requires extensive documentation to establish eligibility. To qualify as an Alien of Extraordinary Ability, the alien must be able to provide evidence of a one-time achievement that is a major internationally recognized award, or at least three other evidences.

Collecting evidence is very important for EB1-Extraordinary Ability (or EB1-EA) immigration category. It requires you to not only provide very strong evidence, but also detailed documentation to prove every claim that you make. You can provide "evidence of a one-time achievement (i.e. a major, internationally recognized award," which is a very high standard. 

Certainly, If you have such an achievement, no other evidence is required. For artists or athletes, it may be possible (for example, an Olympic medal). But for people working in scientific and engineering fields, there are not many such awards even exist. An example of such an award would be the Nobel Prize in Sciences. 

2. Present Powerful Evidence - At Least Three Other Evidence

There are two ways to satisfy the requirements for an EB1-EA immigrant application. The first is receiving a major, internationally recognized award. For those who haven’t won the Nobel Prize or other major awards, the second set of standards is not very difficult to achieve. The USCIS regulations require that an alien applicant fulfills at least three of the following ten standards. Here, meeting three criteria is the minimum requirement. You should present strong evidence for at least three to consider your chance of succeeding in EB1-Extraordinary Ability category.

1) Receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine if the alien was the recipient of prizes or awards: The documentation of the alien's receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor. The description of this type of evidence in the regulation provides that the focus should be on the alien's receipt of the awards or prizes, as opposed to his or her employer's receipt of the awards or prizes.

  • Determine whether the alien has received lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor: Relevant considerations regarding whether the basis for granting the prizes or awards was excellence in the field include, but are not limited to: a) The criteria used to grant the awards or prizes; b) The national or international significance of the awards or prizes in the field; and c) The number of awardees or prize recipients as well as any limitations on competitors (an award limited to competitors from a single institution, for example, may have little national or international significance).

The prizes or awards could include a fellowship, or a competitive assistantship, such as a Fulbright award or a medical fellowship. An award does not have to be in the United States. Many countries have science and technology invention awards, education excellence awards, etc. Be sure to include evidence or documents that indicate and explain that the award is a major award. For it to be considered to be a strong evidence, you have to provide information as to what organization has presented this award, and what is the standing of that organization in your field of endeavor. Awards from the government of a country will have a higher standing. Also, you need to provide information as to how many people you competed against, and what category you got the award in. 

Example could be best paper awards in a conference, and the standing of the conference in a field will determine if that evidence is strong or not. For example, if you got an award in an IEEE International conference, then the evidence would be strong. But if you got the award in a regional/school conference, then it may be a weaker evidence. Patent awards can also be listed. For this, the patent review documents and application documents should be attached. You can present patent awards to supplement your list of awards, but you cannot rely on patents alone to qualify as evidence towards this criterion.

2) Membership in associations in the field which require outstanding achievements as judged by recognized national or international experts

The USCIS adjudicator will determine if the association for which the alien claims membership requires that members have outstanding achievements in the field as judged by recognized experts in that field.

The petitioner must show that membership in the associations is based on the alien being judged by recognized national or international experts as having attained outstanding achievements in the field for which classification is sought. For example, admission to membership in the National Academy of Sciences as a Foreign Associate requires individuals to be nominated by an academy member, and membership is ultimately granted based upon recognition of the individual’s distinguished achievements in original research.

Associations may have multiple levels of membership. The level of membership afforded to the alien must show that in order to obtain that level of membership, the alien was judged by recognized national or international experts as having attained outstanding achievements in the field for which classification is sought.

Relevant factors that may lead to a conclusion that the alien’s membership in the associations was not based on outstanding achievements in the field include, but are not limited to, instances where the alien’s membership was based:

  • Solely on a level of education or years of experience in a particular field;

  • On the payment of a fee or by subscribing to an association’s publications; or

  • On a requirement, compulsory or otherwise, for employment in certain occupations, such as union membership or guild affiliation for actors.

Associations that are open to all members of a given profession can be considered, but associations that limit membership to only the most accomplished members of the profession are certainly more valuable. Collecting evidence for this criterion is somewhat questionable to present strong evidence towards this criterion. For example, a membership in IEEE might have been considered strong evidence previously. But some USCIS Service Center issued letters of Request For Evidence (RFE) stating they would not accept this as strong evidence, because it shows that many new members were added every year, and it does not show if you are outstanding in your field. 

The membership of professional organizations may not be considered as evidence towards this criterion unless you can reach a senior or top level, to which people are nominated based on their achievements and through an election process. If you do present this kind of evidence, you have to attach details of what are the nomination criteria for this membership and accompanying review or recommendation letters for this evidence to be considered. You can get these letters from the review committee by requesting them, even if they do not issue them normally.

3) Published material about the alien in professional major trade publications or other major media: 

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine whether the published material was related to the alien and the alien’s specific work in the field for which classification is sought. The published material should be about the alien relating to his or her work in the field, not just about his or her employer or another organization that he or she is associated with. Note that marketing materials created for the purpose of selling the alien’s products or promoting his or her services are not generally considered to be published material about the beneficiary.

  • Determine whether the publication qualifies as a professional publication or major trade publication or a major media publication: Evidence of published material in professional or major trade publications or in other major media publications about the alien should establish that the circulation (on-line or in print) is high compared to other circulation statistics and show who the intended audience of the publication is, as well as the title, date and author of the material.

The material must concern your work in the field. Publications could range from journals specific to your field, like The Journal of Otolaryngology, to major newspapers, like The New York Times. You are not limited to printed media; a story about you on "60 Minutes" might also fulfill this requirement. Basically, any published materials that specifically introduce your work can be used. The publication has to specifically discuss your work and its importance. Whether the evidence is strong or weak will depend upon where the material was published, or which media it was shown on. For example, if CBS interviews you for your work for a news report, then it will definitely be a strong evidence. 

It is also a strong evidence if a publication of your work appears in a major trade publication showing that your work is not just of scientific importance, but also has practical application in industry. But if a report about your work is published just in your school magazine, then it may or may not be considered as a strong evidence. However, you should not hesitate to give whatever evidence you have. Remember, the criterion requires that the publication is about you and your work. It might be due to your work that you got a report published about you. Therefore, the citations about your research work are clearly not enough to satisfy this criterion.

If your evidence is weak, and you are solely relying on it to make the minimum requirements, then you may get a RFE (Request For Evidence) letter from USCIS Service Center later, and you will have to reply to the RFE questions. So, you should remember to provide as much information as you can about the media and trade publication that your work was published in, and its standing. 

4) Participation on a panel or individually as a judge of the work of others in the field or an allied field

The USCIS adjudicator will determine whether the alien has acted as the judge of the work of others in the same or an allied field of specialization. The petitioner must show that the alien has not only been invited to judge the work of others, but also that the alien actually participated in the judging of the work of others in the same or allied field of specialization. For example:

  • Peer reviewing for a scholarly journal, as evidenced by a request from the journal to the alien to do the review, accompanied by proof that the review was actually completed.

Sitting on the Nobel Prize Committee would fulfill the requirement, as would participating in the peer review process of a scientific article or acting as a member of a thesis review committee. If you have participated as a referee in a professional conference, or if you are an editor of an academic magazine, or if you have reviewed other people's work or research, you can present evidence for this criterion. You may also present comparable evidence if you have participated in an interviewing panel to hire applicants for an academic job or professional job. 

However, as an example, even though you can mention that you have been involved in a teaching profession where you had evaluated students for their work, this does not constitute evidence towards this criterion. USCIS does not accept this king of evidence, and definitely not for undergraduate students. Since the students have not become professionals yet, and they are not your peers. Evaluating post-graduate students, such as a PhD thesis for example, may constitute as evidence.

5) Original scientific, scholarly, artistic, athletic, or business-related contributions of major significance in the field

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine whether the alien has made original contributions in the field. USCIS officers will evaluate whether the original work constitutes major, significant contributions to the field. Although funded and published work may be “original,” this fact alone is not sufficient to establish that the work is of major significance. For example, peer-reviewed presentations at academic symposia or peer-reviewed articles in scholarly journals that have provoked widespread commentary or received notice from others working in the field, or entries (particularly a goodly number) in a citation index which cite the alien's work as authoritative in the field, may be probative of the significance of the alien’s contributions to the field of endeavor.

  • Determine whether the alien’s original contributions are of major significance to the field. USCIS officers will take into account the probative analysis that experts in the field may provide in opinion letters regarding the significance of the alien’s contributions in order to assist in giving an assessment of the alien’s original contributions of major significance. That said, not all expert letters provide such analysis. Letters that specifically articulate how the alien’s contributions are of major significance to the field and its impact on subsequent work add value. Letters that lack specifics and simply use hyperbolic language do not add value, and are not considered to be probative evidence that may form the basis for meeting this criterion.

Basically, the USCIS will base its judgment of your contribution on the letters of support that others in the field submit. So letters from recognized authorities in your field who consider your contributions original and significant will satisfy this requirement. If you have a PhD, then you may be able to argue that your work was original scientific work, since only then you could have received your PhD.

You can provide a list of your publications in national or international scientific and engineering journals which will only publish original work. You can also attach any correspondence which you have had with the reviewers asking for changes to make your work original and worthy of publication in journals. You should also remember to attach the final acceptance letter. Citations of your work may also prove that your research contribution is original, and others are interested in your research too. You can also request other people to write a recommendation letter to specifically mention your research, and how it has made original and significant contributions to the field.

6) Authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in professional or major trade publications or other major media

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine whether the alien has authored scholarly articles in the field. As defined in the academic arena, a scholarly article reports on original research, experimentation, or philosophical discourse. It is written by a researcher or expert in the field who is often affiliated with a college, university, or research institution. In general, it should have footnotes, endnotes, or a bibliography, and may include graphs, charts, videos, or pictures as illustrations of the concepts expressed in the article. For other fields, a scholarly article should be written for learned persons in that field. ("Learned" is defined as "having or demonstrating profound knowledge or scholarship"). Learned persons include all persons having profound knowledge of a field.

  • Determine whether the publication qualifies as a professional publication or major trade publication or a major media publication. Evidence of published material in professional or major trade publications or in other major media publications should establish that the circulation (on-line or in print) is high compared to other circulation statistics and who the intended audience of the publication is.

This refers to articles that you wrote concerning your work rather than material written about you by others, as is the case with the criterion 3 above. Again, the publications can range from major trade journals to mass media. Although the regulations refer specifically to "articles," other forms of publication such as visual media should fulfill this requirement also. 

This criterion might be one of the criteria for which you might have strong evidence, i.e. a list of your publications in major conferences and journals of national or international level. While providing a list of your publications, you may also need to provide evidence of the ranking of the journals or conferences in which you papers was published. For example, SCI Journal Citation Report ranks journals by impact factor in the United States.  In other countries, you might have to locate the databases which provide such evidence. You should give details on how the journals are ranked.

Citations of your work in other researcher's work indicate that your research is useful in your field. You can provide a list of your work's citations. If the citations are not in English, you have to provide a translation. You may also present reprint requests for your publications. You do not have to provide full copies of your publications or citations. The first one or two pages along with the last page which usually has a list of references is good enough.

7) Display of the alien's work at artistic exhibitions or showcases

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine whether the work that was displayed is the alien’s work product. The description of this type of evidence in the regulation provides that the work must be the alien's.

  • Determine whether the venues (virtual or otherwise) where the alien’s work was displayed were artistic exhibitions or showcases.

The regulations do not mention how prestigious the exhibition must be. This criterion is mainly for artists, but you can present comparable evidence for this criterion even if you are working in a scientific or engineering field. For example, there are trade shows in which you can display your work, and you may present one product that you developed in the telecommunication industry in a trade show. It does not have to be your own individual exhibition or showcase. If you just participated in it, you can claim to be a part of it. 

But again, this criterion is mainly for artists. Your evidence should be strong. Presenting a poster in a conference is a weak evidence for this criterion, and a RFE letter from USCIS Service Center may reject your claim because the USCIS believes that you were not the main contributor.

8) Evidence that the alien has performed in a leading or critical role for organizations  or establishments that have a distinguished reputation

The USCIS adjudicator will

  • Determine whether the alien has performed in leading or critical roles for organizations or establishments. In evaluating such evidence, USCIS officers must examine whether the role is (or was) leading or critical. If a leading role, the evidence must establish that the alien is (or was) a leader. A title, with appropriate matching duties, can help to establish if a role is (or was), in fact, leading. If a critical role, the evidence must establish that the alien has contributed in a way that is of significant importance to the outcome of the organization or establishment’s activities. A supporting role may be considered “critical” if the alien’s performance in the role is (or was) important in that way. It is not the title of the alien’s role, but rather the alien’s performance in the role that determines whether the role is (or was) critical.

  • Determine whether the organization or establishment has a distinguished reputation. USCIS officers should keep in mind that the relative size or longevity of an organization or establishment is not in and of itself a determining factor. Rather, the organization or establishment must be recognized as having a distinguished reputation. 

This is one criterion where letters from individuals with personal knowledge of the significance of the alien’s leading or critical role can be particularly helpful to USCIS officers in making this determination as long as the letters contain detailed and probative information that specifically addresses how the alien’s role for the organization or establishment was leading or critical. Evidence of experience “shall” consist of letters from employers.

This could be acting as curator for the Metropolitan Museum of Art or serving as an essential researcher for an important laboratory. Generally, a management position in a scientific organization will be a strong evidence for this criterion. You can also play a critical role in just one department of the organization. The impact of your work or role does not have to be organization-wide. You should keep in mind that the evidence should prove that your role is critical, if you can prove that you are not only responsible for your own work but also for the work of others, or in other words, in a management role.

This can also include the establishment of a commercial company if the company is successful, as well as other non-commercial organizations. You do not have to be the only one to establish such an organization. If you were one of the founding members, you can claim this criterion. As a proof towards this criterion, you can request one or more people writing recommendation letters for you to mention this.

9) Evidence that the alien has commanded a high salary or other high remuneration for services

The USCIS adjudicator will determine whether the alien’s salary or remuneration is high relative to the compensation paid to others working in the field. Evidence regarding whether the alien’s compensation is high relative to that of others working in the field may take many forms. If the petitioner is claiming to meet this criterion, then the burden is on the petitioner to provide appropriate evidence. Examples may include, but are not limited to, geographical or position-appropriate compensation surveys and organizational justifications to pay above the compensation data. Aliens working in different countries will be evaluated based on the wage statistics or comparable evidence in that country, rather than by simply converting the salary to U.S. dollars and then viewing whether that salary would be considered high in the United States.

The regulation requires that your salary or remuneration be high in relation to others in the field, so a teacher needs not make as much as a professional football player. The evidence in this criterion includes the high salary, a high paid speech, a high paid performance, or consultancy services. For higher than the average salary, you may need to provide tax returns or payroll slips. Also, you may need to provide a salary survey report or other such evidence which gives average salaries in your field to compare your salary with others. Also, if you have a higher than average salary, you can provide reference to the survey that Department of Labor used to evaluate the Labor Certification.  

Three Web sites that may be helpful in evaluating the salary:

  • The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS): http://www.bls.gov/bls/blswage.htm 

  • The Department of Labor’s Career One Stop website: http://www.careeronestop.org/SalariesBenefits/Sal_default.aspx 

  • The Department of Labor’s Office of Foreign Labor Certification Online Wage Library: http://www.flcdatacenter.com 

10) Evidence or commercial success in the performing arts, as shown by box office receipts or record, cassette, compact disk, or video sales

The USCIS adjudicator will determine whether the alien has enjoyed commercial successes in the performing arts. This criterion focuses on volume of sales and box office receipts as a measure of the alien’s commercial success in the performing arts. Therefore, the mere fact that an alien has recorded and released musical compilations or performed in theatrical, motion picture or television productions would be insufficient, in and of itself, to meet this criterion. The evidence must show that the volume of sales and box office receipts reflect the alien’s commercial success relative to others involved in similar pursuits in the performing arts.

This can be demonstrated by box office receipts from your films or plays, sales of your record, or selling your video documentary to a network for a notable sum. This criterion has been defined for artists, but you may provide comparable evidence towards this criterion for some cases. Commercial success is very important for this type of success. You do not have to provide evidence about how you achieved your success, but the end result is what it counts. For example, you can present evidence of sales of the product that you developed, or the commercial success of the technique developed by you.

USCIS states that if the above criteria do not readily apply to beneficiary's occupation, the petitioner may submit comparable evidence to establish the beneficiary's eligibility. Without evidence of such a one-time achievement or three criteria demonstrating sustained acclaim, the EB1-EA petition will likely fail. 

Also, technically meeting the initial evidence requirement does not guarantee that the alien will be found to be an alien of extraordinary ability. Satisfying three out of the ten criteria does not guarantee that the USCIS will grant you EB1-EA classification as an alien of extraordinary ability. The USCIS looks for quality as well as quantity. As in many other aspects of immigration law, comprehensive documentation of your qualifications is very important. 

3. The Comparable Evidence to Establish the Beneficiary’s Eligibility

If the standards above do not readily apply to the beneficiary’s occupation, the petitioner may submit comparable evidence to establish the beneficiary’s eligibility. The USCIS adjudicator will determine if the evidence submitted is comparable to the evidence required in regulation.

This regulatory provision provides petitioners the opportunity to submit comparable evidence to establish the alien beneficiary's eligibility, if it is determined that the standards described above do not readily apply to the alien’s occupation. When evaluating such "comparable" evidence, USCIS will consider whether the EB1-EA criteria are readily applicable to the alien’s occupation and, if not, whether the evidence provided is truly comparable to the criteria listed in that regulation.

General assertions that any of the ten objective criteria described in EB1-EA do not readily apply to the alien’s occupation are not probative and may be discounted. Similarly, claims that USCIS should accept witness letters as comparable evidence are not persuasive. The petitioner should explain why it has not submitted evidence that would satisfy at least three of the criteria set forth in EB1-EA, as well as why the evidence it has submitted is “comparable” to that required under EB1-EA criteria.

On the other hand, the following are examples of where comparable evidence might apply:

  • An alien beneficiary who is an Olympic coach whose athlete wins an Olympic medal while under the alien’s principal tutelage would likely constitute comparable evidence.

  • Election to a national all-star or Olympic team might serve as comparable evidence for evidence of memberships in EB1-ER.

Note: There is no comparable evidence for the one-time achievement of a major, international recognized award.
